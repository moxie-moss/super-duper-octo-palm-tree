What Tradeswomen Wish Their Employers Knew About Fit

MT
Maya Torres
June 18, 2026 · 6 min read

When we surveyed 500 women in the trades — electricians, plumbers, carpenters, ironworkers — about their biggest on-the-job frustrations, "workwear that doesn't fit" ranked second only to "being taken seriously." That's not a fashion complaint. It's a safety and productivity issue.

"I can't do a proper squat in the pants I was issued. So I don't. And that means I'm compensating all day and my back pays for it." — Licensed electrician, 8 years experience

The Problem with Men's Sizing

For decades, workwear has been designed for men and scaled down for women. The result is pants with waistbands that gap, inseams that pucker, and seat measurements that are either too tight to crouch or so baggy that extra fabric catches on equipment.

The average woman in the trades wears a different waist-to-hip ratio than the average man. That's not a controversial statement — it's anatomy. But the workwear industry has been slow to catch up.

What Actually Needs to Change

Based on our survey data and two years of fit-testing with tradeswomen across 12 states, here's what women consistently ask for:

  • Waist-to-hip ratio accommodation — without compromising tool pocket access
  • Rise that allows full range of motion — crouching, climbing, kneeling, without the waistband digging in
  • Knee reinforcement positioned for women's body proportions — not men's knee heights scaled down
  • Weight-appropriate fabrics — women run cooler than men; breathability matters differently
  • Pocket placement that actually works — front pockets at thigh level, not hip level

What Employers Can Do

If you're a contractor, site manager, or safety officer reading this: you have more power here than you think. Uniform policies that allow women to source their own properly-fitted workwear — and reimburse accordingly — are a simple first step. Several companies we spoke with have already moved in this direction.

Beyond policy, listening helps. The women on your crews know exactly what's wrong with their gear. Schedule time to actually ask.

A Note on Cost

Properly fitted workwear isn't more expensive — it just seems that way because the default "cheap" options were only ever designed for one body type. When you account for replacement frequency, productivity loss, and injury risk, the economics shift quickly.

One thing is certain: the industry is changing. Tradeswomen are a growing portion of the skilled trades workforce, and workwear brands that don't adapt will lose that market entirely.
